Here's the most common story in pharmacy private services: an owner invests in training, equipment and stock. The team is ready. The service is genuinely good. And six weeks later the diary has four bookings in it, three of them regulars.

The conclusion owners draw — "there's no demand around here" — is almost always wrong. The demand is real and measurable: people in your town are searching for these services every day. They're just booking with whoever they can *find*. Fix findability and bookability, in this order:

## Fix 1: Your Google Business Profile (the big one)

When someone searches "ear wax removal near me", Google shows a map with three businesses. That map pack takes the majority of clicks, and most pharmacies aren't in it for their services — because their profile says "Pharmacy" and nothing else.

The 80/20:

- **Add every private service** to the Services section, with descriptions and prices
- **Photos of the actual service**: consultation room, equipment, (consented) treatment shots — not just your shopfront
- **Use Google Posts** for seasonal pushes (travel season, flu season)
- **Answer the Q&A section** before strangers do it for you
- **Categories**: alongside "Pharmacy", add relevant secondary categories where available

Do this well and you'll often see calls and direction requests rise within weeks — it's the highest-ROI hour in pharmacy marketing. (It's also [a service I run](/services/google-business-profile/) for owners who'd rather not learn Google's quirks.)

## Fix 2: One page per service on your website

Google ranks *pages*, not businesses. A single "Our Services" page listing twelve offerings ranks for none of them. Each money-making service needs its own page: "Ear Wax Removal in [Town]" with prices, method, FAQs and a booking button.

If your [website can't do this easily — or loads like it's 2015](/services/pharmacy-websites/) — that's the underlying problem to fix, because every other marketing effort funnels through it.

## Fix 3: Online booking, or you're closed at 9pm

Check your own behaviour: when did you last phone to book anything? Private service patients decide in the evening, on the sofa, often booking for a parent. Requirements:

- Real-time slots, booked in under a minute, no account creation
- Automatic confirmation and a reminder message (no-shows drop dramatically)
- A deposit or card-capture option for high-value appointments like [travel consultations](/private-services/how-to-start-a-pharmacy-travel-clinic/)

"Call us to book" is a demand filter that keeps only your most determined patients.

## Fix 4: Reviews — the compounding asset

Reviews do two jobs: they rank you (review quantity, recency and keywords are local ranking factors) and they convert (patients choose the 4.9-star clinic over the 4.1). The system:

- Ask at the moment of delight — the ear wax "I can hear!" moment, the traveller collecting their jabs
- Make it one tap: QR card at the counter, or an automatic post-appointment text
- Reviews that mention the service by name ("brilliant ear wax removal") are ranking gold — you can't script them, but asking "would you mind mentioning which service you had?" is fair game
- Reply to every review, including the grumpy ones

One review a week is 50 a year — enough to dominate most local markets within 18 months.

## Fix 5: Recalls — the free bookings you're ignoring

Every past patient is a future booking that requires no marketing spend. Ear wax returns in 6–12 months. Travel vaccines need boosters. [Weight management](/private-services/pharmacy-weight-management-service/) runs monthly. B12 is every 12 weeks. Yet most pharmacies treat every appointment as a one-off and start acquisition from scratch.

A recall message at the right interval ("It's been a year since your ear wax appointment — book here") routinely fills 20–30% of a mature clinic's diary. Set the recall interval at the point of treatment and automate the rest — this is exactly the repetitive, time-based work that [automation and AI agents](/services/ai-agents/) do better than humans with jobs to do.

## The order matters

Don't scatter effort. GBP first (fastest impact), service pages second, booking third, then reviews and recalls as permanent systems. Pharmacies that work through this list in order typically see the difference within one season — and the ones that don't will keep concluding "there's no demand", while their competitor two streets over quietly books another £60 appointment.
