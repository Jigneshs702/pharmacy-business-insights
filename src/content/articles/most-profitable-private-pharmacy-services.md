Dispensing volume is up, margins are down, and the funding contract isn't coming to the rescue. If your pharmacy's profit still depends entirely on the NHS, you don't have a profit problem — you have a business model problem.

The fix is private services. Here's the honest, ranked view of which ones make money in a UK community pharmacy, based on what's actually working — not what looks good in a trade-magazine press release.

## How to read the numbers

All figures are typical UK ranges, and they're **revenue**, not profit — though gross margins on most of these services run 60–90% once you're set up. Your results depend on your location, footfall and (most of all) how well you fill the diary. Want to model your own numbers? ## 1. Travel clinic — the revenue heavyweight

**Typical charge:** £100–£300+ per patient (consultation plus vaccines) · **Setup:** £2,000–£5,000

Nothing else in community pharmacy matches the basket size. A family of four heading to Kenya can be a £900 transaction. Rabies courses, yellow fever (if you register as a centre), hepatitis B — these are high-ticket items patients expect to pay for, because the NHS doesn't cover most travel vaccines.

The catch: it's seasonal (peaks before summer and Christmas), stock management matters, and you need proper training. Full breakdown in the [travel clinic launch guide](/private-services/how-to-start-a-pharmacy-travel-clinic/) and the [travel clinic profit deep-dive](/private-services/pharmacy-travel-clinic-profit/).

## 2. Ear wax removal — the profit-per-hour king

**Typical charge:** £50–£80 both ears · **Setup:** £1,500–£3,000

Since GPs largely stopped providing ear syringing on the NHS, demand has been relentless — and it's evergreen, not seasonal. A microsuction appointment takes 20–30 minutes, consumables cost pennies, and patients come back every 6–12 months if you set up recalls.

Run four appointments a day at £60 and that's over £57,000 a year from one consultation room. See the [ear wax removal service guide](/private-services/ear-wax-removal-pharmacy-service/) for equipment, training and pricing details.

## 3. Weight management — the recurring-revenue engine

**Typical charge:** £150–£250/month per patient · **Setup:** mostly training and SOPs

GLP-1 injections changed everything. Private weight management is the fastest-growing service in pharmacy, and it's **subscription revenue** — a patient on a £199/month plan is worth £2,400 a year. Fifty active patients is a six-figure service.

It's also the most scrutinised: you need robust consultations, genuine wraparound support and airtight clinical governance, not a "cash for pens" operation. The [weight management service guide](/private-services/pharmacy-weight-management-service/) covers doing it properly.

## 4. Private flu and travel-adjacent jabs

**Typical charge:** £15–£25 (flu), £30–£75 (others) · **Setup:** minimal if you already vaccinate

Low ticket, but almost zero marginal cost if you're already running NHS flu clinics — and every private jab is a chance to introduce your other services. B12 injections (£30–£40, often repeat every 12 weeks) quietly build a loyal recurring base.

## 5. Blood tests and health checks

**Typical charge:** £45–£150 depending on panel

Partnering with a phlebotomy lab lets you offer well-person panels, cholesterol, diabetes and hormone checks. Growing fast as NHS waiting lists push patients to self-pay. Margins depend heavily on your lab agreement — negotiate.

## 6. Emergency contraception and period-delay consults

**Typical charge:** £20–£35

Small numbers, but instant, walk-in, and high trust value. Patients who use you once for a discreet, professional private consult come back for bigger things.

## 7. Blood pressure and health MOTs

**Typical charge:** £30–£50

Cheap to run, great upsell path into blood tests and weight management. Works best packaged ("Know Your Numbers MOT") rather than sold as a lone BP check.

## 8. Aesthetics and skin

**Typical charge:** £150–£300 per treatment

Genuinely lucrative, but a different business: significant training, insurance and reputational stakes. Only sensible if a pharmacist genuinely wants to specialise.

## 9. Private prescriptions and PGD clinics

Hair loss, erectile dysfunction, acne, jet lag — small individually, meaningful in aggregate, and largely automatable with a good online front end.

## The pattern nobody talks about

Look at any pharmacy making serious private income and the story is never "they picked a magic service." It's always the same three-part machine:

1. **A service patients actually search for** (everything above qualifies)

3. **A booking path with zero friction** — online booking, reminders, recalls

Most pharmacies get step one right, then wonder why the room sits empty. The service was never the problem. The marketing was.
