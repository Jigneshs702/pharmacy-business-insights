A travel clinic is the highest-basket private service a community pharmacy can offer. One backpacker heading to Southeast Asia can be a £250–£400 patient. A family going on safari can be £900+. And because the NHS doesn't cover most travel vaccines, patients arrive *expecting* to pay.

Here's the full launch path, in order.

## Step 1: Decide your service scope

You don't have to launch with everything. A sensible tiered approach:

- **Tier 1 (launch):** travel consultations, hepatitis A, typhoid, tetanus/diphtheria/polio boosters, antimalarial prescribing via PGD
- **Tier 2 (month 2–3):** rabies, hepatitis B, meningitis ACWY, Japanese encephalitis
- **Tier 3 (when volume justifies):** yellow fever centre registration

Tier 1 alone covers the majority of trips to popular destinations. Rabies is worth adding early — courses are multi-dose and high-value, and pre-exposure demand keeps growing.

## Step 2: Training and PGDs

You need three things:

1. **A travel health foundation course** — teaches risk assessment by destination, itinerary and patient factors. This is the skill that separates a proper clinic from a jab counter.
2. **Injection technique certification** — you likely have this if you deliver flu vaccination.
3. **A PGD package** — Patient Group Directions authorising you to supply and administer each vaccine and antimalarials. Several UK providers sell pharmacy travel PGD packages on annual subscription (typically £300–£800/year depending on scope).

Budget £500–£1,500 per pharmacist for initial training, refreshed annually.

## Step 3: Premises and stock

- **Consultation room** — you already have one; make it feel clinical and tidy, not like a stockroom with a chair.
- **Fridge capacity** — travel vaccines will meaningfully increase your cold-chain volume. Data-logged fridge, alarm, documented daily checks.
- **Stock strategy** — this is where new clinics lose money. Don't fill the fridge with hopeful stock. Order short-dated-risk vaccines (rabies, JE) against booked appointments, and hold routine stock (hep A, typhoid) at modest levels.

## Step 4: Pricing

Check two or three local competitors (chain travel clinics and any pharmacy clinics), then price at or slightly under the chains while emphasising availability. Typical UK prices:

| Item | Typical price |
|---|---|
| Risk-assessment consultation | Free–£20 (often redeemed against vaccines) |
| Hepatitis A | £55–£70 |
| Typhoid | £35–£45 |
| Rabies (per dose, 3 doses) | £60–£90 |
| Hepatitis B (per dose, 3 doses) | £40–£55 |
| Meningitis ACWY | £55–£75 |
| Antimalarials (per course) | £30–£90 |

A free consultation redeemed against treatment removes the biggest booking objection and costs you nothing when the patient proceeds — and most do.

## Step 5: The booking system (don't skip this)

Travel patients plan. They will happily book online at 10pm for next Tuesday — if you let them. You need:

- Online booking with real-time slots (not "call us")
- Automatic confirmation and reminder messages
- A pre-travel questionnaire sent at booking, so consultations run faster

Pharmacies that take travel bookings by phone only are leaving half the demand to whoever ranks next on Google.

## Step 6: Marketing that actually fills the diary

In order of return on effort:

1. **Google Business Profile** — add "Travel clinic" services, travel-specific photos, and posts before peak seasons. Most "travel vaccinations near me" bookings are won or lost here. ([How to win those searches](/private-services/get-more-private-service-bookings/))
2. **A dedicated travel clinic page on your website** — one page per major search ("travel vaccinations [your town]").
3. **Counter conversations** — every antimalarial query, sun cream purchase and EHIC question in spring is a travel clinic lead. Script it for your team.
4. **Seasonality** — push hardest January–June (summer trips) and September–November (winter sun, Christmas VFR travel).

## What it makes

Realistic maths for an established suburban clinic: 6 patients a week at an average £110 basket, 48 weeks = **£31,680/year**, at 65–75% gross margin. Busy urban clinics with strong Google presence do several times that.
