Weight management is the fastest-growing private service in UK pharmacy, and it's not close. GLP-1 medicines created something pharmacy has rarely had: a high-value service patients actively seek out, delivered monthly, on subscription.

It's also the service with the highest scrutiny. Regulators have made clear that "cash for pens" operations — questionnaire, payment, medication, goodbye — are not acceptable. The pharmacies winning here run genuine programmes. Good news: the genuine programme is also the more profitable one, because it retains patients.

## The business case

The unit economics are unlike anything else in pharmacy:

- **£150–£250/month per patient**, medication included
- **30–50% margin** after drug costs, consultations and admin
- **Patients stay months, often a year+** — a patient at £199/month for ten months is a **£1,990 customer**
- **50 active patients ≈ £120,000/year** of recurring revenue

Compare that with [every other private service](/private-services/most-profitable-private-pharmacy-services/): travel is high-basket but transactional; ear wax repeats yearly. Weight management compounds monthly. Model your version in the [profit calculator](/tools/profit-calculator/).

## What "doing it properly" means

Build the service around four pillars — they're both the compliance answer and the commercial answer:

1. **Real clinical assessment.** Face-to-face or video consultation, BMI verified (not self-declared), full history, contraindication screening, and honest triage — including telling unsuitable patients no. Prescribing sits with a pharmacist independent prescriber or a partnered prescribing service.
2. **Structured onboarding.** Injection technique teaching, side-effect counselling (nausea management alone prevents half of early dropouts), expectation setting, and written plans.
3. **Monthly reviews that earn the fee.** Weight, side effects, dose titration, and actual lifestyle input — diet quality, protein intake, activity. This is what separates a programme from a supply chain, and it's why patients stay.
4. **An exit ramp.** Maintenance planning and off-ramping support. Patients who feel safely managed refer their friends; patients who feel sold to don't.

## Pricing: package, don't itemise

Sell a **monthly plan** (medication + consultations + support + check-ins) at £150–£250 depending on drug and dose — not a consultation fee plus a drug price plus extras. Packages are simpler to buy, hide drug-cost volatility, feel like a programme, and materially outperform itemised pricing on retention.

Offer a paid initial assessment (£25–£50, redeemable against the first month) rather than free consultations — it filters for committed patients and protects clinical time.

## Marketing a sensitive service

Weight management marketing has rules — both regulatory (prescription-only medicines can't be advertised to the public by name) and reputational. What works within them:

- **Market the service, not the drug.** "Pharmacist-led weight management programme" — never drug names in ads, social posts or your Google profile.
- **Own the local search.** "Weight management pharmacy [town]" and related searches go to whoever has the strongest [Google Business Profile](/services/google-business-profile/) and a proper service page.
- **In-pharmacy conversations.** Your team already talks to patients about blood pressure, diabetes and joint pain daily. A trained, sensitive one-line signpost ("we run a pharmacist-led weight programme, would you like a leaflet?") is the highest-converting channel you have.
- **Reviews with care.** Happy patients will review enthusiastically; guide them toward mentioning the *service and support* rather than medication specifics.

## The retention machine

Revenue in this service is retention. The operational playbook:

- Automated monthly review booking (the patient never has to remember)
- Check-in messages between reviews, especially weeks 2–6 when side effects bite
- Missed-appointment chasing within 24 hours
- Refill coordination so nobody has a gap week (gap weeks are where patients quit)

This is repetitive, time-sensitive communication — which is why it's the single best use case for [an AI agent in a pharmacy](/services/ai-agents/). Pharmacies that follow up systematically keep patients months longer than those that rely on the patient calling back, and months of retention is where the entire profit lives.

## Start small, but start structured

Launch with a capped cohort — say 15 patients — run the full programme properly, gather outcomes and reviews, then scale. A waiting list is better marketing than a discount, and the operational lessons from 15 patients are a lot cheaper than from 150.
