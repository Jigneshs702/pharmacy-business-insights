Choosing ear wax removal training is the first real decision in launching [pharmacy's best profit-per-hour service](/private-services/ear-wax-removal-pharmacy-service/) — and the market is a confusing mix of excellent clinical educators and slick sales operations. Here's how to choose well, without naming-and-shaming: the criteria matter more than the brand.

## What a course must include (non-negotiables)

1. **Otoscopy taught properly.** Recognising a healthy tympanic membrane, perforations, infection and exostoses is the actual skill. Wax removal is easy; knowing when *not* to proceed is the training.
2. **Hands-on practical assessment.** On live models or real patients, with an assessor signing off your competence. Theory-only certificates are not enough for your indemnity insurer — check with them before booking anything.
3. **Both microsuction and irrigation.** Even if you'll lead with microsuction, you want both tools and the judgement of when each is appropriate.
4. **Contraindication and referral frameworks.** Written criteria for when to refuse, refer or seek advice.
5. **Post-course support.** The scary moments happen in week three with a tricky ear, not in the classroom. Mentorship access afterwards is worth paying for.

## What it costs and what that buys

| Tier | Price | What you get | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| E-learning only | £100–£300 | Theory certificate | Not sufficient alone |
| Standard practical course | £800–£1,200 | Theory + 1 day practical assessment | The sensible minimum |
| Premium / mentored | £1,200–£2,000 | Theory + practical + equipment guidance + ongoing mentorship | Worth it for a first clinician |

If you're training multiple team members, most providers discount heavily for group bookings — and training a technician alongside the pharmacist doubles your clinic capacity for a fraction of double cost.

## Seven questions to ask any provider

1. Is the practical assessment on real ears or models, and how many procedures do I perform before sign-off?
2. Is the course accredited, and by whom? Will it satisfy the main pharmacy indemnity providers?
3. Does it cover both microsuction and irrigation?
4. What post-course support exists when I hit a difficult case?
5. Do you help with SOPs, consent forms and record templates, or just the clinical skill?
6. Do you sell equipment too — and if so, am I free to buy elsewhere? (Bundles can be good value; compulsory bundles are a red flag.)
7. Can you connect me with a pharmacy already running the service?

That sixth question matters: some training operations make their real money on marked-up equipment. The training can still be good — just price the bundle against buying kit independently before signing.

## The timeline from booking to earning

A realistic path: book the course (week 0), complete e-learning (weeks 1–2), practical day (weeks 3–4), set up room, SOPs and insurance notification (weeks 4–6), first paying patients (weeks 6–8). At [typical clinic economics](/private-services/ear-wax-removal-pharmacy-service/), most pharmacies recover the entire training and equipment cost within the first 6–10 weeks of a modestly busy clinic.
